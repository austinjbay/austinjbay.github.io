AI is changing growth strategy, but not in the neat, dramatic way people sometimes talk about it.

It’s not just that teams can make more ads, write more emails, or ship more landing pages. That’s part of it, sure. But the bigger shift is that AI changes the cost of trying things. And when the cost of trying things drops, strategy itself starts to move.

A lot of growth work used to be constrained by production. You had ideas, but limited time, limited creative, limited bandwidth, limited research capacity. Now many of those constraints are softer than they were even a year ago.

That doesn’t mean growth got easier. It means the shape of the work is changing.

## Strategy matters more when execution gets cheaper

One of the stranger effects of AI is that it makes it easier to confuse output with progress.

When content, copy, creative variations, messaging drafts, research summaries, and campaign ideas can be generated quickly, teams can feel productive without getting much closer to actual growth. Activity expands. Learning does not always keep up.

So the real leverage moves upstream.

The teams that benefit most from AI are not the ones producing the most assets. They’re the ones with the clearest point of view about:

- who they are trying to reach
- what problem matters most
- what belief needs to change
- where friction exists in the funnel
- what signal they are looking for from the market

If execution becomes abundant, judgment becomes scarce.

That’s the part I keep coming back to: AI raises the premium on taste, prioritization, and strategic clarity.

## Growth is becoming more about learning velocity

I think one of the most useful ways to view AI in growth is as a tool for increasing learning velocity.

Not just shipping velocity.

Shipping more is only useful if it helps you understand something faster:

- which message resonates
- which audience converts
- which channels produce durable demand
- which product moments create activation
- which objections keep showing up

AI can help compress the cycle between question, test, insight, and iteration.

You can go from a rough hypothesis to a set of message variants, landing page directions, audience angles, interview prompts, and analysis frameworks much faster than before. For small teams especially, that’s a big deal.

But faster loops also create a new risk: running lots of shallow tests that don’t build toward any coherent understanding.

Without a strong learning agenda, speed creates noise.

## The old growth advantages are getting weaker

There were a lot of tactical advantages that used to matter more:

- being able to produce more content than competitors
- launching more campaign variations
- quickly rewriting ad copy
- spinning up SEO pages at scale
- personalizing outbound faster
- repackaging the same core message into many formats

AI is making all of that more accessible.

That’s good in one sense. More teams can operate with leverage. Small teams can do work that used to require specialists or agencies. You can get from zero to decent much faster.

But it also means “more” stops being a durable edge.

If everyone can create passable content and reasonable copy, then average execution gets flooded. Distribution gets noisier. Attention gets harder to earn. Differentiation matters more.

So growth strategy shifts away from pure volume and toward sharper decisions:

- what not to say
- which audience to ignore
- which jobs to serve exceptionally well
- which category assumptions to challenge
- where product truth is strong enough to build a narrative around

AI lowers the cost of generic work. It does not automatically create specificity.

## Messaging gets easier to generate and harder to get right

This is one of the most interesting changes.

AI is very good at producing plausible messaging. It can generate clean headlines, value propositions, campaign hooks, nurture sequences, and positioning drafts in seconds.

That’s useful. I use it for exactly that kind of work.

But plausible messaging is not the same as sharp messaging.

Sharp messaging usually comes from a deeper mix of things:

- real customer language
- pattern recognition across conversations
- product intuition
- category context
- knowing what to emphasize and what to leave unsaid

AI can help synthesize inputs, but it still tends to flatten the edges unless you bring strong material into the process. If the source inputs are vague, the outputs usually sound polished and forgettable.

That means growth teams need to get closer to raw customer signal, not further from it.

The better your notes, interview excerpts, sales call patterns, support themes, win/loss observations, and product usage context, the better AI becomes as a strategic amplifier.

Garbage in, generic out.

## The best use of AI is often before and after the campaign

A lot of the conversation centers on campaign production, but I think the more interesting use cases are around the edges.

Before a campaign, AI can help with:

- synthesis of research
- audience segmentation ideas
- message territory exploration
- drafting hypotheses
- identifying gaps in the funnel
- turning scattered notes into a test plan

After a campaign, AI can help with:

- summarizing qualitative feedback
- clustering themes across survey responses
- comparing performance narratives across channels
- turning experiment results into follow-up questions
- documenting what was learned

In other words, AI is valuable not only as a maker of assets, but as a partner in sensemaking.

That matters because good growth strategy is not just making things. It’s deciding what things are worth making, and understanding what happened after they went live.

## Product and growth are moving closer together

This was probably happening anyway, but AI seems to accelerate it.

As growth teams get better tooling for content, analysis, experimentation, and user understanding, the line between product work and growth work gets blurrier. More growth questions start to look like product questions:

- Where does value become obvious?
- What moment creates conviction?
- Which friction blocks activation?
- What behavior predicts retention?
- What promise can the product actually keep?

AI doesn’t remove the need for product strategy. It makes weak product-growth alignment more visible.

If acquisition gets more efficient but activation is still muddy, the bottleneck shows up faster. If messaging improves but the onboarding experience doesn’t support the promise, the gap becomes clearer. If you can test more top-of-funnel angles but the product still lacks a compelling core loop, growth has less room to hide.

This is healthy, honestly.

It pushes growth away from surface-level channel management and closer to the full user journey.

## Teams will need better operating systems, not just better prompts

A lot of advice about AI still lives at the prompt level.

That’s fine, but it feels incomplete.

The more durable advantage is probably not “who wrote the best prompt.” It’s “which team has the best system for turning AI into repeatable learning.”

That system might include:

- clear ICP definitions
- a shared message architecture
- documented funnel hypotheses
- a lightweight experimentation process
- a clean way to capture customer voice
- reusable analysis templates
- strong feedback loops between product, growth, and sales

AI works better when it plugs into structure.

Without that structure, it often just creates a larger pile of drafts, summaries, and loosely connected ideas. Helpful in moments, but not compounding.

With structure, it can help teams think more clearly and act more consistently.

## Human taste is becoming a strategic asset

I don’t mean taste in the aesthetic-only sense, though that matters too.

I mean taste as the ability to notice what is interesting, true, specific, and worth amplifying.

When average content becomes cheap, taste becomes a filter:

- Which insight is actually non-obvious?
- Which message feels lived-in instead of manufactured?
- Which story is worth repeating?
- Which detail makes the claim believable?
- Which experiment is worth the team’s attention?

AI can generate possibilities. Humans still need to decide which possibilities feel honest and useful.

That’s especially important in growth, where there is always pressure to overstate, overproduce, and over-optimize. The temptation with AI is to scale all of that.

But the teams I trust most will probably use AI to get more precise, not more inflated.

## What I think good growth teams will do differently

My current take is that strong growth teams will use AI in a few grounded ways.

They’ll use it to reduce low-value labor, yes. But more importantly, they’ll use it to improve how they think.

They’ll:

- test more ideas without pretending every test matters equally
- get closer to customer language instead of further away from it
- spend less time formatting and more time interpreting
- connect channel data to product reality
- build stronger internal memory around what has been tried and learned
- stay skeptical of easy volume

And maybe most importantly, they’ll keep asking better questions.

Because that still feels like the real bottleneck.

Not whether a team can generate ten landing pages in an afternoon.

But whether they know what they’re trying to learn, what they believe about the market, and what kind of growth they actually want to build.

## A practical way to start

If I were tightening a growth workflow around AI today, I’d start small.

Pick one recurring growth problem, something like:

- campaign brief creation
- customer research synthesis
- landing page iteration
- ad concept generation
- churn feedback analysis
- post-experiment documentation

Then build a simple loop:

1. Gather real inputs from customers, funnel data, and team context.
2. Use AI to create first-pass options or synthesis.
3. Review with a human point of view, not just for polish but for truth.
4. Ship a focused test.
5. Capture what happened.
6. Feed the learning back into the next round.

That seems more useful than trying to “AI transform” the whole function at once.

Small loops compound.

## Closing thought

I don’t think AI replaces growth strategy.

I think it exposes it.

It exposes whether a team really understands its customer, whether it has a clear point of view, whether it can tell signal from noise, and whether it can turn motion into learning.

The surface layer of growth is getting automated quickly.

The deeper layer — judgment, insight, positioning, sequencing, restraint — still looks very human.

Maybe even more human than before.
