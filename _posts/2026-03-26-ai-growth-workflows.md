## The shift I keep noticing

A lot of teams are still using AI like a faster search box.

That’s not nothing. It helps with summaries, drafts, brainstorming, cleanup work. But it’s also a pretty limited frame. It treats AI as a tool you visit, instead of a system you design into how work happens.

The more interesting shift, at least from a growth and product perspective, is thinking in workflows.

Not “how do I get a better prompt?”

More like:

- Where does work currently get stuck?
- What decisions are repetitive but still require judgment?
- What signals are scattered across tools, docs, dashboards, and customer conversations?
- What would it look like to turn that mess into a repeatable loop?

That’s the part that feels durable to me.

## A workflow is the unit, not the prompt

Prompts matter, but they’re not the whole thing.

A useful AI growth workflow usually has a few parts:

- an input
- some context
- a transformation
- a decision
- an output
- a human checkpoint

That sounds obvious, but it changes how you design the work.

For example, instead of saying “use AI to write lifecycle emails,” you might define a workflow like this:

- Pull recent activation drop-off patterns
- Pull support tickets from new users
- Pull notes from sales calls with stalled prospects
- Summarize likely friction points
- Generate 3 onboarding email variants tied to those friction points
- Have a PMM or lifecycle lead review and tighten claims
- Ship to a test segment
- Feed results back into the next draft

That’s not magic. It’s just better plumbing.

And better plumbing tends to beat one-off cleverness.

## Good AI workflows usually start with boring problems

The strongest use cases are often not flashy.

They live in the gaps:

- turning customer feedback into themes
- drafting experiment briefs
- creating first-pass positioning options
- spotting patterns in failed conversions
- generating test ideas from qualitative research
- repackaging one core insight into multiple formats
- preparing weekly growth reviews
- translating dashboard movement into plain language

These are all places where teams lose time, not because the work is hard in some heroic sense, but because it’s fragmented.

A lot of growth work is coordination work.

AI can help stitch together context that normally lives in too many places and arrives too late.

## The real value is compression

What I like most about AI workflows is that they compress the distance between signal and action.

Normally the loop looks something like this:

1. data shows something weird
2. someone notices a week later
3. someone asks for more context
4. another person pulls call notes or support tags
5. a meeting gets scheduled
6. three hypotheses emerge
7. nobody writes them down clearly
8. an experiment eventually gets queued

That’s a lot of drag.

A decent workflow can compress pieces of that into a faster, more legible system:

- detect the change
- gather nearby context
- summarize likely explanations
- suggest next tests
- route to the right owner

Not to remove thinking. To make thinking happen sooner.

## Where I think teams get it wrong

One trap is trying to automate the most strategic part first.

That usually backfires.

The fuzzier the problem, the more important taste, context, and judgment become. If you hand that whole layer over to AI, you often get work that looks finished before it’s actually useful.

Another trap is treating output volume as progress.

More ideas are not always better ideas. More copy is not better positioning. More experiments are not better learning.

A lot of AI-generated work creates a strange management tax. Someone still has to review it, prune it, and decide what matters. If the workflow creates more surface area than clarity, it’s probably not helping.

The third trap is building workflows with no clear owner.

If nobody owns the loop, it degrades fast. Inputs break. Context gets stale. Outputs become generic. People stop trusting it.

Trust is the whole game here.

## What makes a workflow actually useful

The best workflows I’ve seen have a few qualities in common.

### They start from a real decision

Not “generate content.”

More like:

- Should we change onboarding messaging for this segment?
- Which activation friction is most likely worth testing next?
- What objections are showing up often enough to warrant a sales asset?
- Which win-loss themes deserve a homepage update?

When the decision is clear, the workflow has a job.

### They pull from live context

Generic inputs produce generic outputs.

If you want a workflow to be helpful, it needs real material:

- recent customer calls
- product analytics
- support conversations
- CRM notes
- campaign performance
- roadmap context
- prior experiments

This is where most of the leverage lives. Not in fancier prompting, but in better context assembly.

### They preserve a human editor

I don’t mean a ceremonial approval step.

I mean a real person who can say:

- this pattern is real
- this is overfit
- this claim is too strong
- this misses the segment nuance
- this is technically true but strategically dumb

That layer matters a lot, especially in growth, where local maxima are everywhere.

### They feed learning back into the system

A workflow should get better over time.

If a generated experiment idea was weak, note why. If an AI summary missed the core insight, adjust the context. If a messaging draft performed well, capture what was actually resonant.

Otherwise you’re just rerunning a static machine.

## A simple place to start

If I were setting this up from scratch on a lean team, I’d start with one weekly workflow:

### Weekly growth insight synthesis

Inputs:

- product dashboard changes
- top support conversations
- sales call notes
- experiment results
- customer research snippets

Process:

- summarize what changed
- identify repeated friction or demand signals
- propose 3 to 5 hypotheses
- suggest 1 to 2 low-cost tests
- draft a short weekly memo

Human review:

- growth lead or PM reviews the memo
- sharpens what matters
- deletes weak inferences
- assigns follow-ups

Outputs:

- one readable artifact
- one decision list
- one small set of next actions

This sounds simple because it is simple.

But simple, consistent synthesis is weirdly rare. And when a team gets it right, the compounding effect is real. People stop debating what happened and start discussing what to do.

## The hidden benefit: shared language

One thing I didn’t expect is how much AI workflows can improve internal language.

When teams repeatedly synthesize customer pain points, objections, motivations, and experiment learnings in the same structure, they start to speak more clearly.

You get fewer vague statements like “users are confused” and more useful ones like “new users understand the setup flow, but they don’t trust the data connection step.”

That kind of specificity helps across product, marketing, design, and sales.

The workflow becomes a forcing function for clearer thinking.

## My current heuristic

A workflow is worth building when:

- the task happens often
- the inputs are messy
- the decision matters
- the first draft is time-consuming
- a human can reliably judge quality
- the loop can improve with feedback

If those conditions are true, AI can be a real multiplier.

If not, you may just be adding novelty.

## What I’m trying to remember

AI doesn’t remove the need for judgment.

If anything, it raises the premium on it.

When more things can be drafted, summarized, mapped, remixed, and suggested instantly, the scarce thing becomes discernment. Knowing what to ignore. Knowing what is signal versus style. Knowing which insight deserves action.

That’s why I keep coming back to workflows.

They’re a better container for practical AI than the endless chase for the perfect prompt. They connect tools to decisions. They make learning easier to reuse. They create small systems that help teams move with more clarity.

That feels more grounded to me than most of the hype.

And probably more useful too.
