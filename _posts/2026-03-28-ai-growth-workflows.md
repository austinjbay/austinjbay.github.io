Growth work is full of awkward handoffs.

A strategy becomes a brief. A brief becomes copy. Copy becomes a landing page. A landing page becomes an experiment. Then someone has to look at the results, figure out what actually happened, and decide what to do next.

AI is useful in that messy middle.

Not because it replaces taste, judgment, or customer understanding. Mostly because it helps reduce the drag between thinking, making, and learning.

That’s the frame I keep coming back to: AI is less interesting to me as a magic answer machine, and more interesting as workflow infrastructure.

## The real opportunity is in the seams

A lot of AI conversation still centers on outputs.

Write the email. Generate the ad variations. Summarize the interview. Draft the PRD.

That’s fine, and often genuinely helpful. But the bigger win usually comes from improving the transitions between steps:

- turning raw research into a sharper problem statement
- turning a strategy doc into testable hypotheses
- turning experiment results into reusable learnings
- turning scattered team context into something easier to act on

The seams are where a lot of growth work slows down. Not because people are bad at their jobs, but because modern work creates too much partial context.

There’s always one more Slack thread, one more Notion page, one more spreadsheet with a slightly different version of the truth.

AI can help compress that chaos into something more usable.

## A simple AI growth workflow

The most practical pattern I’ve seen looks something like this:

1. **Collect signals**
2. **Structure the problem**
3. **Generate options**
4. **Refine with human judgment**
5. **Ship faster**
6. **Capture what was learned**

That probably sounds obvious. It is. That’s part of why it works.

The mistake is usually trying to jump straight from “we have data” to “AI, give me the answer.”

In practice, AI gets better when the workflow around it gets better.

## Step 1: collect signals, not just data

Growth inputs are usually fragmented:

- interview notes
- support tickets
- win/loss feedback
- session recordings
- search query data
- campaign performance
- CRM notes
- sales call snippets
- activation funnels
- onboarding drop-off points

On their own, these are just artifacts.

A useful AI workflow starts by pulling those signals together around a real question:

- Why are qualified visitors not converting?
- What objections keep showing up before purchase?
- Where does onboarding feel unclear?
- Which message angles are resonating, and for whom?

That question matters more than the model.

Without a clear question, AI tends to produce polished ambiguity. It sounds smart while saying very little.

## Step 2: structure the problem before generating ideas

This is the step teams skip.

Before asking AI for solutions, I’ve found it more useful to ask for structure:

- What themes show up repeatedly?
- What assumptions are we making?
- What information is missing?
- Which user segments seem meaningfully different?
- What are the likely bottlenecks in this funnel?
- What hypotheses does this evidence support, and which does it not?

This changes the interaction completely.

Instead of using AI like an intern asked to “come up with some ideas,” you’re using it more like a thinking partner that helps organize the problem space.

That doesn’t remove the need for product sense. It makes product sense easier to apply.

## Step 3: generate options, not a single answer

Once the problem is framed, AI becomes much more useful for divergence.

This is where it can help create:

- messaging angles for different audiences
- homepage headline directions
- onboarding experiments
- lifecycle email drafts
- paid ad variants
- survey questions
- landing page outlines
- test ideas tied to a specific funnel stage

What I want here is range.

Not “the best copy.” Not “the winning strategy.” Just credible options that expand the space a bit faster than I would on a blank page.

That speed matters. Especially in growth work, where momentum is fragile and teams often stall because every next step feels expensive.

AI lowers the cost of exploring.

## Step 4: human judgment is still the product

This is the part worth protecting.

AI can produce decent variants. It can cluster themes. It can summarize patterns. It can help pressure-test a hypothesis.

But it does not know:

- which tradeoffs matter most right now
- what your brand can actually say credibly
- where the politics are in the org
- what customers will perceive as generic
- what quality bar is required for this moment
- when a “good” idea is actually a distraction

That layer is still deeply human.

In growth, judgment often shows up as sequencing:

- Which test is worth running first?
- Which insight is strong enough to act on?
- Which message is clear without becoming simplistic?
- Which opportunity is real versus merely available?

I don’t think AI removes that work. I think it increases its importance.

Because when idea generation gets cheaper, discernment becomes more valuable.

## Step 5: ship faster, but don’t automate sloppiness

One of the easiest traps is using AI to produce more assets than the team can learn from.

More ads. More emails. More pages. More experiments.

That can look productive while actually muddying signal.

The better use is often tighter:

- get from brief to first draft faster
- reduce the time it takes to adapt a message across channels
- create structured experiment plans
- draft QA checklists
- summarize decisions and next steps
- turn rough thinking into something the team can react to

Speed helps when it compounds learning.

Speed hurts when it just compounds volume.

## Step 6: close the loop on learning

This is probably the most underrated use case.

A lot of teams run experiments, launch campaigns, and ship onboarding changes without building a real memory of what happened.

The result is familiar: six months later, someone proposes the same idea again, and nobody remembers whether it failed, worked, or was never actually tested properly.

AI can help turn messy outputs into reusable learning:

- summarize experiment results
- compare expected vs actual outcomes
- extract likely reasons a test underperformed
- identify recurring themes across multiple experiments
- draft a short “what we learned” note for the team
- organize learnings by audience, funnel stage, or message type

This matters because growth is not just execution. It’s accumulation.

The teams that get better are usually the teams that remember better.

## A few workflows that feel immediately useful

Here are a few patterns that feel practical right now.

### Research synthesis to messaging

Take interview notes, sales objections, and support conversations. Ask AI to cluster repeated pains, desired outcomes, and buyer language. Then use that synthesis to draft homepage messaging or campaign concepts.

The win is not that AI writes the final copy.

The win is that it helps bridge customer language into execution without losing a week in the process.

### Funnel diagnosis to test backlog

Feed in funnel metrics, known drop-off points, qualitative observations, and any recent changes. Ask AI to identify likely bottlenecks, propose hypotheses, and generate experiment ideas sorted by funnel stage.

This is especially useful when the team has lots of observations but no clear shape to them.

### Campaign analysis to iteration plan

Take performance summaries from a campaign and ask AI to pull out what seems to have resonated, what didn’t, what segments behaved differently, and what should be tested next.

Again, not as truth. As structured interpretation.

That distinction matters.

### Positioning consistency across surfaces

Once a message strategy is set, AI can help adapt it across:

- landing pages
- email flows
- ad concepts
- in-product prompts
- sales enablement
- help center updates

This is less glamorous than “generate a strategy,” but often more valuable. A lot of growth friction is really consistency friction.

## What tends to go wrong

A few failure modes keep showing up.

### Teams ask for output before context

If the prompt starts with “write me a landing page,” the result will usually be fine and forgettable.

If it starts with actual customer pain, segment context, product constraints, and desired action, it gets much better.

### Teams use AI to avoid decisions

Sometimes the hidden hope is that AI will resolve ambiguity.

Usually it just reflects it back in cleaner prose.

If the strategy is fuzzy, the outputs will be fuzzy too.

### Teams mistake fluency for insight

This one is dangerous because the writing can sound confident.

A smooth summary is not the same as a sharp conclusion. A plausible hypothesis is not the same as evidence.

You still have to think.

### Teams scale production before they scale learning

This is the growth version of local optimization.

If AI helps you produce 5x more experiments but your review process, instrumentation, and decision-making stay weak, you don’t get smarter. You just get busier.

## The prompt is not the whole system

There’s a lot of attention on prompt writing, which makes sense. Better prompts help.

But in a team setting, the workflow matters more than the cleverness of any single prompt.

What seems to matter most:

- clear inputs
- a specific job to be done
- enough context to narrow the space
- explicit constraints
- a review layer with real judgment
- a way to store and reuse what was learned

That’s the system.

The prompt is just the interface.

## A practical standard I keep coming back to

For most growth work, I think the question is simple:

**Did AI help the team move from ambiguity to action with better speed or clarity?**

Not:
- Did it generate a lot?
- Did it sound smart?
- Did it save ten minutes on a draft?

Those things are nice. But the real value is whether it helps a team:

- understand the problem better
- create stronger options
- reduce execution drag
- preserve learnings
- make better decisions over time

That’s a much more grounded bar.

## Final thought

I don’t think AI changes the fundamentals of growth.

You still need customer understanding. You still need strategy. You still need good taste, clean execution, and the discipline to learn from what you ship.

What it changes is the cost of moving between those steps.

And that matters.

Because a lot of growth work isn’t blocked by a lack of ideas. It’s blocked by friction: too much context, too many handoffs, too little time to synthesize what the team already knows.

That’s where AI feels most useful to me.

Not as a replacement for the work.

As a way to keep the work moving.
