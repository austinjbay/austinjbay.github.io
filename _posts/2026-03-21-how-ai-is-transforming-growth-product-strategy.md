Growth used to be constrained by a pretty simple set of limits: how fast your team could ship, how much data you could reasonably analyze, and how many experiments you could keep in flight without the whole system turning into noise.

AI is changing those limits.

Not in the magical, "push a button and growth happens" way. More in the practical sense that some work is getting cheaper, some decisions are getting faster, and some product surfaces that used to feel too manual to scale now look viable.

I’ve been thinking about AI less as a feature category and more as a shift in the cost structure of growth. When the cost of creating, testing, and adapting experiences drops, strategy has to change too.

## Growth strategy is moving closer to the product

For a long time, growth strategy often sat in a familiar operating model: acquisition on one side, product on the other, lifecycle somewhere in between, and a lot of handoffs connecting the pieces.

AI compresses some of that distance.

When a product can adapt onboarding in real time, generate contextual assistance, or personalize activation paths based on behavior, growth stops being just about moving people into the product. It becomes more about shaping the product experience itself.

That matters because many of the best growth opportunities now live inside the product:

- helping users reach value faster
- reducing the effort required to get started
- matching the experience to intent
- making the next useful action obvious
- recovering moments where users would otherwise stall

That’s not entirely new. But AI makes these moves more dynamic and more scalable than they used to be.

## Personalization is becoming operational, not aspirational

A lot of teams have talked about personalization for years. In practice, most of it ended up being lightweight segmentation, a few lifecycle branches, and some manual rules that were expensive to maintain.

AI changes the equation by making it easier to respond to messy, high-volume signals.

Instead of asking, "Which onboarding flow should this segment get?" teams can start asking better questions:

- What is this user actually trying to do?
- What friction are they likely to hit next?
- What piece of guidance would help right now?
- What level of complexity is appropriate for this person today?

That doesn't mean every product should turn into a black box that improvises the whole experience. It does mean that more teams can build systems that are responsive without requiring endless manual tuning.

The practical shift is this: personalization is becoming something teams can operate continuously, not just something they plan in strategy decks.

## Experimentation gets faster, but judgment matters more

One obvious effect of AI is speed.

Teams can generate variants faster, summarize user feedback faster, identify patterns faster, and reduce the manual overhead around experimentation. That should be good news for growth teams.

But faster experimentation has a downside: it becomes easier to test a lot of things without really learning much.

When idea generation is cheap, discernment becomes more valuable.

The bottleneck moves from "Can we produce enough experiments?" to questions like:

- Are we testing meaningful hypotheses?
- Are we improving the user experience or just chasing local lifts?
- Are we measuring durable behavior or short-term clicks?
- Are we creating trust, or quietly eroding it?

I think this is one of the bigger strategic changes. AI increases leverage, but it also increases the risk of flooding the system with shallow optimization. Teams that win won’t just be the ones running more tests. They’ll be the ones with a clearer model of what good growth looks like.

## The best AI growth work often looks like better UX

Some of the most valuable AI work in growth won’t announce itself as AI.

It will look like:

- onboarding that feels less generic
- empty states that are actually useful
- search that understands intent better
- lifecycle messages that sound timely instead of automated
- upgrade moments that connect to real value
- support experiences that unblock users before frustration hardens

This is important because AI can tempt teams into novelty. Chat interfaces, copilots, and generated everything are easy to get excited about. Sometimes those are the right moves. Sometimes the better move is much quieter.

If AI helps a user get to value in two minutes instead of ten, that’s growth strategy.
If it helps a team identify where users get stuck and fix the underlying product, that’s growth strategy too.

Not every meaningful AI investment needs to become a headline feature.

## Retention may be the biggest opportunity

A lot of AI conversations still skew toward acquisition and top-of-funnel efficiency. That makes sense. The gains are visible, and the tools are easy to demo.

But I think the deeper opportunity is retention.

If AI helps products become more adaptive, more helpful, and less effortful, then the compounding impact shows up in whether people keep coming back. Retention improves when the product keeps earning its place in someone’s workflow.

That can happen through:

- smarter guidance as users mature
- better defaults and recommendations
- context-aware education
- faster time-to-value for new use cases
- lower friction in recurring tasks
- recovery paths when users go inactive

In other words, AI can help products behave more like attentive systems and less like static software.

That has real growth implications. Strong retention makes every other motion work better.

## The strategy question is no longer "Should we use AI?"

For most teams, that’s not the useful question anymore.

The more useful questions are:

- Where in the journey is human effort currently too high?
- Where are users forced to translate their intent into our rigid product structure?
- Where do teams rely on manual rules that don’t scale well?
- Which moments would improve if the product could better understand context?
- Where would adaptation create value, and where would it create confusion?

That framing helps because it starts from user friction and operating constraints, not technology theater.

The goal isn’t to add AI because the market expects it. The goal is to remove friction, increase relevance, and make the product more capable of helping people succeed.

## AI changes team design too

This part feels under-discussed.

As AI gets embedded into growth work, the lines between product, data, design, engineering, and marketing get even blurrier. The work becomes more cross-functional by default.

A growth PM may need to think more like a systems designer.
A marketer may need to think more about product behavior.
A designer may need to shape how adaptive experiences remain legible and trustworthy.
A data partner may move closer to decision systems instead of just reporting.

The teams that adapt well will probably share a few traits:

- they stay close to user behavior
- they instrument the product thoughtfully
- they treat prompts, models, rules, and UX as one system
- they review outputs with the same care they apply to shipped product
- they know where automation helps and where humans should stay in the loop

This is less about becoming "AI-first" and more about becoming better at integrating intelligence into the way the product grows.

## Trust is a growth constraint

AI can absolutely improve conversion and activation.

It can also make a product feel confusing, intrusive, or unreliable if it overreaches.

Growth teams have always had to balance persuasion with trust, but AI raises the stakes. When products infer intent, generate content, or adapt experiences dynamically, users need to understand what’s happening well enough to stay oriented.

A few questions I think are worth asking:

- Does this behavior feel helpful or pushy?
- Is the system confident where it should be, and cautious where it should be?
- Can users recover easily if the AI gets it wrong?
- Are we making the product easier to use, or harder to predict?
- Are we optimizing for metrics in a way that weakens long-term trust?

This is not just an ethics sidebar. It’s core growth strategy. Trust affects activation, retention, expansion, and brand. Once it slips, it’s expensive to earn back.

## A practical way to think about it

If I were looking at AI through a growth product lens, I’d roughly bucket opportunities into three areas:

1. **Make the team faster**
   - speed up analysis
   - generate experiment variants
   - synthesize research
   - improve operational throughput

2. **Make the experience more adaptive**
   - personalize onboarding
   - tailor guidance
   - improve discovery
   - respond to context in-product

3. **Create new value for the user**
   - automate hard tasks
   - reduce expertise required
   - unlock outcomes that weren’t previously practical

That third category matters most.

Using AI to accelerate existing growth work is useful. Using AI to make the product genuinely more valuable is where the strategy gets more durable.

## What probably won’t change

Even with all of this shifting, a few things still look pretty stable to me.

Good growth strategy still starts with understanding user motivation.
Clear positioning still matters.
Strong product fundamentals still matter.
Retention is still harder than acquisition.
And no model can compensate for a product that doesn’t solve an important problem well.

AI changes the tools, the speed, and the shape of the opportunities.
It does not remove the need for taste, judgment, and a clear view of what value actually means for the user.

## Closing thought

The interesting thing about AI in growth is that it’s both overhyped and genuinely important.

A lot of noisy ideas will come and go. Some teams will bolt AI onto weak experiences and call it strategy. Some experiments will create activity without creating value.

But underneath the hype, something real is happening: products are becoming more responsive, teams are getting more leverage, and growth strategy is becoming more tightly coupled to the actual user experience.

That feels like the shift worth paying attention to.

Not AI as decoration.
AI as a way to build products that learn faster, help more, and reduce the distance between user intent and user value.
